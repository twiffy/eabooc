In this assignment, you will learn specific guidelines for making both performance and portfolio assessments. You will make either a performance assessment or a portfolio assessment.

  • To fully succeed with this assignment, read Chapter 8 and Chapter 9 in Popham’s text Classroom Assessment: What Teachers Need to Know.
  • This is a more complicated assignment than the previous one. You will need to return to some of the earlier parts because they will not fully make sense until after your work on the later parts.
  • You are responsible for the guidelines and advantages and disadvantages of both formats, even though you will only create one assessment.
  • This is a detailed assignment. Use your time wisely. There is a lot of ground to cover here and you want to make sure you save time for commenting and discussion.  In particular, don't use up all of your time reading the chapters if you think you will be short on time.  
  • This is a good time to begin looking for outside resources in your domain and context to supplement the text.  You can find a LOT of useful examples and ideas by searching on Google.  The best performance and portfolio assessments are the ones that have been tried out and refined by thoughtful teachers or researchers. so look for those.
  • You are encouraged to put links to outside resources in your wikifolios and to post generally useful links to the discussion forum called Performance and Portfolio Assessment Resources.

  1. Make sure your students “own” their own portfolios.
  2. Decide on what kinds of work samples to collect.
  3. Collect and store work samples.

Create Scoring Rubrics

Use the guidelines for scoring rubrics in Chapter 8 to create three scoring rubrics for your performance or portfolio assessment.  Read Who is Evaluating Whom and Appraising Portfolios in Chapter 9 (even if you created a performance assessment).  

Draft (or describe if it is too complex) each of the following types of scoring rubrics for your performance task.  

  • Task-Specific Rubric (Popham, p. 217).  These are rubrics where the evaluation criteria are solely connected to the task the students perform during the assessment.  Discuss Popham's critique of task-specific scoring rubric.  Do you agree with him?
  • Hypergeneral Rubric (Popham, p. 218). This is what many refer to as a "holistic rubric."  Add a sentence or two commenting on Popham's critique of hypergeneral rubrics.  Do you agree?
  • Skill-Focused Rubric (Popham, 219). Draft a skill focused rubric, following the five rules. Then, indicate which two of the five rules seem most important to YOU and why. Again, your domain and role should be articulated here as relevant. Specifically, you should begin to consider scoring from the perspective of an instructional leader, administrator, or researcher.

Rule 1: Make sure the skill to be assessed is significant.

Rule 2: Make certain all of the rubric’s evaluative criteria can be addressed instructionally.

        Rule 3: Employ as few evaluative criteria as possible.

Rule 4: Provide a succinct label for each evaluative criterion.

Rule 5: Match the length of the rubric to your own tolerance for detail.

Obviously Popham prefers skill-focused rubrics.  Do you agree with him?   Are there any downsides to them?

Evaluate your Assessment

Evaluate your performance or portfolio assessment according to each of the seven evaluative criteria in Chapter 7. Review the criteria first and place them in order of relevance for you.  Order them as best you can but at minimum explain why the first is most relevant and the last is least relevant.

Write a brief paragraph about each criteria. You may revise your assessment in light of the criteria as you go but it is not necessary. But you should certainly describe how your assessment might be changed to better reflect the criteria. If you do revise it, consider explaining what you’ve changed and why. You might have to stretch the Chapter 7 criteria a bit to fit Portfolio Assessment but that is okay.  Here is a summary of the criteria:

  • Generalizability: Is there a high likelihood the students’ performance on the task or activity will generalize to a comparable task or activity?
  • Authenticity: Is the task or activity similar to what students might encounter in the real world as opposed to encounter only in school?
  • Multiple foci: Does the task or activity assess multiple instructional outcomes instead of only one?
  • Teachability: Is the task or activity one that students can become more proficient in as a consequence of a teacher’s instructional efforts?
  • Fairness: Is the task or activity fair to all students--that is, does it avoid bias based on such personal characteristics as students’ gender, ethnicity, or socioeconomic status?
  • Feasibility: Is the task or activity realistically implementable in relation to its cost, space, time, and equipment requirements?
  • Scorability: Is the task likely to elicit student responses that can be reliably and accurately evaluated?
