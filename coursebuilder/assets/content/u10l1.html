EVALUATION OF INSTRUCTION AND TEACHERS

This assignment introduces you to the evaluation of instruction and teachers. If you are not in a context that directly lends itself to this assignment, use some context that is close enough and adapts the assignment so that you can make sense of the concepts outlined in the instruction and the referenced Pondertime questions. If your context does not allow you to apply them then say so and try to explain why.

About the landscape: Teacher accountability is changing quickly. Chapter 15 in the Seventh Edition was probably written in 2012, just as the edition was going to press. Things have already changes some since them, and are going to change more. This assignment is prepared to bring up to speed with the current situation and prepare you to make sense of the many changes to come. This assignment is also intended to help teachers, administrators, and researchers learn to talk to each other about these potentially contentious topics. There will be tensions as states simultaneously introduce new standards, tests, and accountability schemes. There may be mayhem. You need to be prepared!

1. Read/Review Chapter 15 and watch videos. Read the chapter, and make sure you have a sense of the two evaluative guidelines, the five test prep practices, and the nuances of teaching to the test. It is a short chapter so you should read it straight through, while considering how these things mean different things for different assessment formats, domains, and roles.

Watch videos by Linda Darling-Hammond (central to the SBAC coalition) on Value Added Measures . Watch this video from the Aspen Institute on the Role of Teacher Evaluation in Reforming Public Schools

2. INSTITUTIONAL ROLE. Make a subpage and summarize your current and/or future role in the educational ecosystem relative to evaluation of instruction and teachers. How do you or might you evaluate your own instruction? How will your instruction be evaluated? Will you be compared to other teachers? Will you compare teachers to each other?

3. LESSON SUMMARY. Summarize your lesson used in the previous assignments as it might be used in the next scenario. You may want to consider a broader swath of the curriculum. Be sure to summarize the curricular aims.

4. EVALUATING INSTRUCTION. Describe how you might use classroom assessment evidence to evaluate your instruction. Describe both designs in the book and indicate which is most appropriate and the advantages and disadvantages for you.

Make some assertions about evaluating instruction in your context. Be sure to touch on Pondertime 2 and 3. And keep in mind that time spent taking tests may or may not directly contribute to student learning.

5. EVALUATING TEACHERS. Summarize what you know about how teachers are currently evaluated in your context, and how this is going to change in the coming years. You may have to do some digging and some of you will surely rely on your classmates for help. This is the part of the course where the future standouts will be most apparent to the class. Feel free to link to resources that you have located on the web that are relevant to you.

Make some assertions and have some opinions. Be sure to touch on (a) instructional sensitivity, (b) student growth percentiles, (c) instructional insensitivity, and (d) value added models, and (e) instructionally-insensitive items. Be sure to touch on Pondertime 1 and 5.

6. BIG IDEAS EVALUATING INSTRUCTION AND TEACHERS. Review What to Classroom Teachers Need to Know and the Summary. State the three most relevant big ideas about evaluating instruction and teachers for you and people in your role. Make it clear why the first one is most relevant.

7. Move up your wikifolio subpage. Once you are ready for your classmates and the instructor to comment, drag and drop a copy of the subpage up under the appropriate section so your classmates and the instructor know it is ready to be discussed

8. Discussion (before and after deadline). Post at least one question to the instructor and your classmates, but post it as a comment rather than in the body so readers will see it readily. After drafts are up, go in and look at each others's wikifolios and leave comment or suggestions at the bottom. Be sure to check back and answer any questions or responses.

9. REFLECTIONS (within one week of the original deadline). In order to receive credit for each wikifolio, you must post three brief reflections at the bottom of the wikifolio. You must have a coherent reflection that demonstrate understanding of the concepts and how they related to your lesson context.

Consequences. What are the actual or potential consequences of learning the ideas in this chapter for your educational practice? Can you think of anything you might do differently after reading this chapter and completing this assignment? Emphasize things that you learned about assessment and about your lesson from reading and discussing the chapter.

Critiques. Now that you have a better understanding of these ideas about assessment, how good was your lesson, setting, and role for learning these things? Did one of your classmates have a better context for learning some of these concepts? Why? Note that you are NOT being asked to critique or improve your lesson or your assessments. You are being asked to consider the appropriateness of your lesson as a context for applying and learning the big ideas in the chapter.

Collaboration. Whose comments and examples were particularly helpful for learning the ideas in this chapter? Be specific and name people and what they contributed that was helpful.

You are advised to begin drafting the reflections while you are working on the wikifolio, and then completing them before starting the next assignment. They should serve a review and help you better connect the ideas of the chapter to your practice.
