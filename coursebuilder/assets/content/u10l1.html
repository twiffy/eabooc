Consider Using Classroom Assessment Evidence

Instruction is often evaluated by comparing what students know before the instruction to what they know after the instruction is given. Many educators have never actually evaluated their own instruction in this way.  Many who do are surprised at what they discover. Students often do surprisingly well on pretests; in many classrooms, some students will score higher on pretests than other students score on the post-test.  More generally, the use of pretests often reveals that students are not learning nearly as much as teachers would otherwise think they were learning.

 

There are two primary ways of using evidence from classroom assessments to evaluate instruction:

 

  • Pretests Versus Posttests.  This is where you give the same assessment before and after instruction.  As explained in the book and here this is easy but problematic
  • Split-and-Switch Design.  This is sometimes called “split-half” and uses a technique called counter-balancing.  As explained in the book and here (scroll down) it is more effective but can be challenging to do.

Consider Instructional Sensitivity

One of the biggest challenges when evaluating instruction is having items that are sensitive to the curricular aim of the instruction.  Even when items are aligned to the aim, they are often too easy or too hard for some (or even all) students.   Popham does a nice job simplifying instructional insensitivity on pages 389-393, and then list six causes of instructionally insensitive items on 397-398.  

 

Popham discusses instructional sensitivity in the context of external accountability tests, but instructional sensitivity is also a big challenge for classroom assessments.  The challenge is somewhat different for the pre-posttest design using classroom assessments than it is for external accountability tests.  Consider these sources of insensitivity in the context of whichever kind of evidence (classroom or accountability) you ranked as most relevant above.

 

  • Alignment Leniency: When the test item is not aligned to (does not measure) the curricular aim thought to be measured. This means the item is measuring something other than the curricular aim.
  • Excessive Easiness: The test item is so easy that students who were not even taught will answer the item correctly; so the test item does not measure student achievement on the curricular aim.
  • Excessive Difficulty: The test items are so difficult that even students who mastered the curricular aim will not answer it correctly; so the item does not distinguish between well-taught and poorly taught students.
  •  Item Flaws: Errors in the test item itself cause students to answer the item incorrectly; so the item does not distinguish between well-taught and poorly taught students.
  • Socioeconomic Status Links: When a test item unfairly advantages students from higher SES statuses and so the item measures the knowledge students bring to school rather than how they are taught within the classroom.
  • Academic Aptitude Links: When a test item unfairly advantages students coming to the classroom with already higher quantitative, verbal, or spatial aptitudes and so the item measures the knowledge students bring to school rather than how they are taught within the classroom.

Evaluating Teaching and Teachers

“In the last few years we have seen an enormous increase in the clamor for test-based teacher evaluation--that is, teacher appraisals in which the dominant (but not the only) determiner of a teacher’s quality is the performance of a particular teacher’s students on an externally imposed standardized achievement test” (Popham, p. 384).

 

Popham has a lot of opinions about test-based teacher evaluation in Chapter 15 and in this Oregon Live column.  Popham’s big point in the chapter is that teachers should be evaluated using instructionally sensitive tests in concert with teacher made assessments.  The challenge is that making accountability tests sufficiently “objective” for making high-stakes evaluations of teachers (as the US and many countries are trying to do) makes them less sensitive to a particular teacher’s instruction.  

 

On page 386-387 Popham describes student growth percentiles introduced in the US with the 2002 No Child Left Behind act.  He also describes some of the problems with using student growth percentiles.  On pages 396-397, Popham describes the new value-added models which proponents say address the shortcomings of student growth percentiles.  Popham argues that value added models are “regarded by most non-partisans as inappropriate for evaluating the quality of educators’ endeavors”  (p. 396).  For now in the US and some other countries, the “partisans” of value-added modeling have prevailed.  Value-added evaluation of teachers is central element of the US Race to the Top initiative.  Most states in the US belong to one of two large coalitions, which are developing new tests and value-added accountability policies that will be phased in over the coming years.

 

If value-added policies impact your work, you need to be familiar with the techniques and policies.  Read the sections in Popham carefully, and then review the following resources on teacher evaluation.
