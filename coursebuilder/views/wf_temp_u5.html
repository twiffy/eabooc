Overview

In this assignment, you will learn about reliability (the consistency of scores from a given assessment) and assessment bias (whether an assessment is biased against individuals or groups) by applying those concepts to the assessments you already created.

With this unit, we begin the second part of the course.  In contrast to the relatively concrete guidelines for creating assessments, you are now going to start dealing with more abstract principles. You will see in this unit that there are no clear right or wrong answers. If you still can't come up with anything to say after reading your classmates entries, then you are free to indicate that in your entries. These are difficult questions, and some of you will struggle more than others.  But you will return to these issues throughout the semester. You will also begin searching for relevant outside resources that consider these ideas in your context, role, and domain.

This unit is based on Chapters 3 and 5 in the textbook. For further information on bias, it might be helpful to watch this video from the author of our textbook, Popham.

Reiterate Your Context (By October 13)

Highlight the aspects of your lesson and role that are particularly relevant to these issues. You should probably revisit and rewrite after you have completed the rest of the assignment.  For example, many low-stakes classroom assessments do not need to be particularly reliable, but high-stakes tests used to make important decisions need to yield consistent scores; assessment bias is probably more likely in a social studies class than a chemistry class.

Reflection (BY OCTOBER 20)

Post three brief reflections. You should write a coherent reflection that demonstrates understanding of the concepts and how they related to your curricular aims and context. On your Wikifolio, be sure to label each of the three types of engagement as you present your reflection.

A. Consequential Engagement. What might you do differently after reading about this week’s topic and completing this assignment?

B. Critical Engagement. How well-suited was your role and curricular aim to learning this week’s concepts and big ideas?  Did one of your classmates have a better context for learning some of these concepts?  Note that you are NOT being asked to critique how well you did.  Rather you are reflecting on the fit between your aim and role and the concepts we learned this week.

C. Collaborative Engagement. Whose comments and examples were particularly helpful for your completing this assignment?  Be specific and name people and what they contributed that was helpful.

You are advised to begin drafting the reflections while you are working on the Wikifolio, and then to complete them before starting the next assignment.  They should serve as a review and help you better connect the ideas of the chapter to your practice.
