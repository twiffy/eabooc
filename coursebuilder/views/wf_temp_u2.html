Overview

In this assignment, you will  learn some "commandments" for writing test items in general and the specific guidelines for four common selected-response formats.  You will do so by creating some constructed response items for your curricular aim. Consider the following:

  • In this assignment, you will use most of the key concepts from Chapter 6 in Popham’s text Classroom Assessment: What Teachers Need to Know. To really master these ideas, read the entire chapter in the context of completing the assignment.
  • You are not necessarily making items you will actually use.  Rather you are creating items to learn about the commandments, formats, and guidelines.  You will do so by figuring out which more relevant to your role and aim.
  • You are expected to become familiar with the items and guidelines for all four item formats, even though you are only required to make items for two (the second two are optional elements).

Item-Writing Guidelines

From Popham, Classroom Assessment, 7th Edition. Consider these summaries, and the guidelines for writing each kind of item.

Binary-Choice Items (p. 161) can be completed very quickly, but they can encourage and reward memorization, and students have a 50% chance of guessing the right answer.
  • Phrase items so that a superficial analysis by the student suggests a wrong answer.
  • Rarely use negative statements, and never use double negatives.
  • Include only one concept in each statement.
  • Have an approximately equal number of items representing the two categories being tested.
  • Keep item length similar for both categories being tested.

Multiple Choice-Binary Items (p. 164) are difficult to create, but can get at deeper understanding, are more reliable, and can be repurposed.
  • Separate item clusters vividly from one another.
  • Make certain that each item meshes well with the cluster’s stimulus material.

Multiple-Choice Items (p. 166) are familiar and can include responses that cover a range of relative correctness, but it can be difficult to make ones that work really well.
  • The stem should consist of a self-contained question or problem.
  • Avoid negatively stated stems.
  • Do not let the length of alternatives supply unintended clues.
  • Randomly assign correct answers to alternative positions.
  • Never use “all-of-the-above” alternatives, but do use “none-of-the-above” alternatives to increase item difficulty.

Matching Items (p. 172) are compact and readily scored, but encourage and reward memorization of surface knowledge.
  • Employ homogeneous lists.
  • Use relatively brief lists, placing the shorter words or phrases to the right.
  • Employ more responses than premises.
  • Order the responses logically.
  • Describe the basis for matching and the number of times responses may be used.
  • Place all premises and responses for an item on a single page.
