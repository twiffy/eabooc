Curricular Aim

Refine your CURRICULAR AIM. Read about curricular aims and what-to-assess considerations in Chapter 2.  Popham describes the “curricular aim” as the desired outcomes of instruction. This is not the “educational objectives,” “instructional objectives,” and “behavioral objectives,” but rather the measurable learning goals. In very simple terms, a Curricular Aim can be viewed as a skill or  a body of knowledge that teachers plan for their students to acquire.  You should definitely not provide a lesson or a project as your curricular aim and you should probably not use an affective outcome as your curricular aim. You should define what Popham describes as a “broad scope” curricular aim.  (see Curricular Aim FAQs)

Concepts

Relevent Educational Standard

Locate a RELEVANT EDUCATIONAL STANDARD. Locate a relevant set of educational standards and identify the standard that is most relevant to your Curricular Aim. If you are a K-12 teacher this should be pretty straightforward. If not you will have to be creative.  If you are a US K-12 Teacher you should almost certainly use the new Common Core or the Next Generation Science standards.  If your state is transitioning, you should consider both existing state standard AND a common core standard. But there are standards out there for almost everything and you should be familiar with them if there are.  You may have to use Google and dig around.

After you locate a standard, copy and paste the actual standard into your Wikifolio page. Then, add a link to the standard and say a few things about it. Try to explain the scope and grain size (e.g., “one of ten standards that make up....”).  Be sure to mention whether the standard is a content standard or a performance standard (see Popham, Chapter 2. This webpage will also provide a simple distinction between the two).  If you are a US math or language arts teacher, read about the common core standards and say a few things about your standard.
